Machine learning¶

  • Nearly all machine-learning problem can be posed as optimisation problems applied to data.

  • We define a loss function: $L: (\mathbf{x}, F_\mathbf{w}) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$

where $\mathbf{x}$ is some data, and $F_\mathbf{w}(\mathbf{x})$ is an arbitrary function parameterised by numeric weights $\mathbf{w}$.

  • $F$ can be non-linear and/or stochastic.

  • We minimise the expected loss by choosing appropriate weights:

\begin{equation} \underset{\mathbf{w}}{\operatorname{argmin}} E[L(\mathbf{x}, F_\mathbf{w})] \end{equation}
  • In statistical learning we sometimes use the word "coefficient" instead of "weight", but the meaning is identical.

Supervised versus unsupervised learning¶

  • In a supervised learning problem we have some additional training data $\mathbf{y}$.

  • The training data provides a finite subset of observed values $\mathbf{y}$ from an unknown function $F$, such that $\mathbf{y} = F(\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon$

where $\epsilon$ are iid. random noise.

  • We want to generalise from the training data to derive the complete mapping $F$.

  • This can then used to generalise to unseen cases where no training data is available.

  • In a supervised learning problem therefore, our loss function takes the form $L: (\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \hat{F}_\mathbf{w}) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$.

  • Intuitively, we should define a loss function that tells us how good the fit is between the function $\hat{F}$ and the observations of $F$.

Statistical and machine-learning¶

  • Standard methods in statistics can also be viewed as optimisation problems.

  • For example, in ordinary least-squares estimation (OLS) the loss function is simply the sum of the squared residuals (RSS):

\begin{eqnarray} L(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{w}) & = & \sum_{i=1}^n r_i^2 \\ r_i & = & y_i - F_\mathbf{w}(x_i) \\ F_\mathbf{w}(\mathbf{x}) & = & \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{w} \end{eqnarray}

  • In the above, the loss function is a simple linear function.

  • The OLS loss function is very straightforward to minimize using calculus.

  • In general, however, machine-learning methods allow us to work with arbitrary non-linear functions.

Regression methods¶

Simple linear regression¶

  • Consider a vector $\mathbf{x}$ containing samples from our predictor, and a vector of $\mathbf{y}$ of corresponding responses, both of size $n$.

  • We assume a stochastic linear relationship between response and predictor: $y = F(x) = \alpha + \beta x + \epsilon$,

  • Our weight vector simply consists of our estimated intercept and slope: $\mathbf{w} = (\hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta})$

  • Our predictions are given by:

\begin{equation} \hat{F}_{(\hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta})} = \hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta} x \end{equation}
  • The residuals are the differences between our predictions and the observed training response:
\begin{equation} r_i = y_i - F_\mathbf{w}(x_i) \end{equation}
  • The loss function $L$ is the residual sum of squares (RSS):
\begin{equation} L(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^n r_i^2 \end{equation}

Assumptions¶

  1. $F(x)$ is linear.

  2. The noise term is i.d.d. normally distributed with a mean 0 and constant variance:

$$\epsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\epsilon})$$
  1. The independent variable is uncorrelated with the noise $E[x_{ij}\epsilon_i] = 0$.

Least squares estimates¶

  • We want to maximise the goodness-of-fit by minimising the expected loss:
\begin{equation} \underset{\mathbf{w}}{\operatorname{argmin}} E[L(\mathbf{x}, F_\mathbf{w})] \end{equation}

where $\mathbf{w} = (\hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta})$

  • In this case we can use calculus to obtain a closed-form solution without resorting to computational methods:
\begin{eqnarray} \hat{\beta} & = & \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x}) ^ 2} \\ \hat{\alpha} & = & \bar{y} - \hat{\beta} \bar{x} \end{eqnarray}

where $\bar{x}$ and $\bar{y}$ are the sample means.

Applications to Finance - The Single Index Model¶

$$r_{i,t} - r_f = \alpha_i + \beta_i ( r_{m,t} - r_f) + \epsilon_{i,t}$$$$\epsilon_{i, t} \sim N(0, \sigma_i)$$
  • $r_{i,t}$ is return to stock $i$ in period $t$.
  • $r_f$ is the risk-free rate.
  • $r_{m,t}$ is the return to the market portfolio.

Elton, E. J., & Gruber, M. J. (1997). Modern portfolio theory, 1950 to date. Journal of Banking and Finance, 21(1112), 17431759. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0378-4266(97)00048-4

Computing the correlation matrix¶

  • For random variables $X$ and $Y$, the Pearson correlation coefficient is:
\begin{eqnarray} \rho_{X,Y} & = & \frac{\operatorname{cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} \\ & = & \frac{E[(X - \mu_x)(Y - \mu_Y)]}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}\\ \end{eqnarray}

  • Notice that the optimal values of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ can be expressed as functions of sample means.

Estimation error¶

  • By the law of large numbers $\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \bar{\mathbf{x}} = E(X)$.

  • However, for finite values of $n$ we will have an estimation error.

  • Can we quantify the estimation error as a function of $n$?

Computing the error numerically¶

  • If we draw from a standard normal distribution, we know that $E(X) = 0$.

  • Therefore we can easily compute the estimation error in any given sample.

The sampling distribution of the mean¶

  • If $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ then $\bar{\mathbf{x}_n} \sim N(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n})$.

  • The standard error of the mean is $\sigma_{\bar{\mathbf{x}}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$.

  • Therefore sample size must be quadrupled to achieve half the error.

Quantifying the variance of a model¶

  • We can quantify the goodness-of-fit of our model when we train it on different data sets.

  • Consider some out-of-sample data $x_0 = 10$ and $y_0 = f(x_0) + \epsilon$.

  • Over the different training sets, we can estimate:

    • the variance of our forecasts: $\operatorname{Var}(\hat{f}(x_0))$,

The bias-variance trade-off¶

  • The additional source of error in the previous example is called the bias.

  • Bias arises when our model is of the wrong form to be able to fully fit the data.

  • In general:

\begin{equation} E[y_0 - \hat{f}(x)]^2 = \operatorname{Var}[\hat{f}(x)] + \operatorname{Bias}[\hat{f}(x)]^2 + \operatorname{Var}[\epsilon] \end{equation}

where

\begin{equation} \operatorname{Bias}[\hat{f}(x)] = E[\hat{f}(x)] - f(x) \end{equation}

Improving the fit the model¶

  • We cannot eliminate the final term in the previous equation; $\operatorname{Var}(\epsilon)$ is irreducible error.

  • In general, if we reduce bias, we increase variance.

  • This is called the bias-variance trade-off.

  • We can reduce variance by reducing the number of weights in $\mathbf{w}$.

  • In multiple regression this can be done by reducing the number of predictors.

  • Correspondingly as we increase the number of predictors we increase variance, but reduce bias.

  • This can result in over-fitting to the training data.

Basis Functions¶

  • We can use non-linear basis functions to decrease the bias of our model.

  • We transform a variable $X$ into $b_1(X), b_2(X), \ldots, b_K(X)$

  • We then fit the model:

\begin{equation} y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 b_1(x_i) + \beta_2 b_2(x_i) + \beta_3 b_3(x_i) + \ldots + \beta_Kb_K(x_i) + \epsilon_i \end{equation}

  • The basis functions are known and chosen.

  • The above equation is still linear in the basis functions.

Multiple linear-regression¶

  • Simple regression can be generalized to multiple predictors.

  • Consider $p$ distinct predictors with corresponding observations of size $n$: $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_p$, then:

\begin{equation} \mathbf{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathbf{x}_1 + \beta_2 \mathbf{x}_2 + \ldots + \beta_p \mathbf{x}_p + \epsilon \end{equation}
  • In matrix notation:
\begin{equation} \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X} \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{\epsilon} \end{equation}\begin{equation} \begin{pmatrix} Y_1 \\ Y_2 \\ \vdots \\ Y_n \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & X_{11} & X_{21} & \ldots & X_{p1} \\ 1 & X_{12} & X_{21} & \ldots & X_{p2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ldots & \vdots \\ 1 & X_{1n} & X_{2n} & \ldots & X_{pn} \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \beta_1 \\ \beta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \beta_n \\ \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_1 \\ \epsilon_2 \\ \vdots \\ \epsilon_n \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}
  • The closed-form solution to the loss-minimization problem is:
\begin{equation} \hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{y} \end{equation}

Potential problems with linear-regression¶

Non-linearity of the response-predictor relationships.¶

  • If the actual relationship between response and predictor is non-linear then linear regression with give a very biased result.

Correlation or non-constant variance in error terms.¶

  • We assume that the $\epsilon$ variate is i.i.d.

  • If there are correlations in $\epsilon$, linear regression can give misleading results.

  • This often occurs in time-series data.

  • Similarly if the variance of the residuals is not constant.

  • As we saw with the single-index model fit, we can examine the plot of the residuals for patterns.

Outliers and high-leverage points¶

  • Extreme values of response variables are called outliers.

  • They can be analyzed using box-plots.

  • Outliers can cause over-fitting.

  • Extreme values of predictor variables are called high-leverage points.

Collinearity.¶

  • We assume there are no linear relationships between predictors.

  • If there are, then we are using redundant information, which can make the model hard to interpret.

  • Collinearity can be detected by looking at scatter matrices.

  • In general, combinations of variables can exhibit a linear relationship, which is called multi-collinearity.

  • Multi-collinearity can be detected by computing the Variable Inflation Factor (VIF).

  • If there are redundant variables, then typically we want to omit them from the regression.

  • Finding the correct set of predictor variables is called variable selection.

Variable-selection and regularization¶

  • We can address some of these issues using penalized regression techniques.

  • Penalized regression can be used to reduce the variance of a model through regularization.

  • It can also be used to eliminate redundant variables.

  • This is particularly important for "big-data" problems with large numbers of predictor variables $p$;

    • for an example in Finance, see the WRDS database.

  • Provided the number of observations is significantly greater that the number of preditors $n > p$, ordinary least squares estimation can give low-variance estimates.

    • If $n < p$ there is no single optimal solution to the OLS optimization problem.

Shrinkage using Ridge regression¶

  • Recall that in linear regression our loss function $L$ is simply the residual sum of squares (RSS):
\begin{equation} \operatorname{RSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n r_i^2 \end{equation}

where

\begin{eqnarray} r_i & = & y_i - F_\mathbf{w}(x_i) \\ & = & y_i - w_0 - \sum_{j=1}^p w_j x_{ij} \end{eqnarray}
  • In ridge regression we add an additional shrinkage penalty term:
\begin{equation} L(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{w}) = \operatorname{RSS} + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p w_j^2 \end{equation}

where $\lambda$ is a tunable hyper-parameter.

The optimal weights are:

\begin{equation} w^* = (\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{y} \end{equation}

  • The lasso regression uses an $\mathscr{l_1}$ penalty term:

  • In contrast to ridge regression, the lasso does not shrink all coefficients to zero for the same $\lambda$.

  • This means that it can be used for variable selection.

Penalized regression as constrained optimization¶

  • An alternative formulation of penalized regression is to view it as constrained optimization.

  • We minimize the standard OLS loss function:

$L(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{w}) = \operatorname{RSS}$

  • For ridge regression we use the constraint:
$$\sum_{j=1}^p w_{j}^2 \leq s$$
  • For lasso regression we use the constraint:
$$\sum_{j=1}^p |w_{j}| \leq s$$
  • For every $\lambda$ there is a corresponding constant budget $s$ for which solutions to the constrained and unconstrained problem are identical.

Lasso and Ridge comparision¶

  • Lasso is able to perform variable-selection when the data-set contains many redundant features that do not influence the response.

Elastic-net¶

  • We can combine both penalties into a single term by using a linear combination.

  • This transforms the problem into a multi-objective optimisation problem (MOO).

  • The parameter $\alpha$ specifies the weighting towards a given objective.

\begin{equation} L(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{w}) = \operatorname{RSS} + \lambda \left( \frac{1 - \alpha}{2} \sum_{j=1}^p |w_j| + \alpha \sum_{j=1}^p w_j^2 \right) \end{equation}

Kernel methods¶

  • Recall that we can sometimes we can fit to non-linear data using basis functions.

  • We use a set of functions that increases the number of the features, e.g.:

\begin{equation} \{ 1, x_1, x_2, x_3, x_1x_2, x_1x_3, x_2 x_3, x_1^2, x_2^2, x_3^2 \} \end{equation}
  • Our set of basis functions is $ \{ \phi_m(\mathbf{x})\}_{m=1}^{M_\phi} $
\begin{eqnarray*} \phi_1(\mathbf{x}) & = & 1 \\ \phi_2(\mathbf{x}) & = & x_1 \\ \ldots \\ \phi_6(\mathbf{x}) & = & x_1 x_3 \\ \ldots \\ \end{eqnarray*}

Model¶

\begin{equation} F_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{m=1}^{M_\phi} w_m \phi_m(\mathbf{x}) \end{equation}

Features¶

\begin{equation} \mathbf{\Phi} = \begin{pmatrix} \phi_1(\mathbf{x}_1) & \cdots & \phi_{M_{\phi}}(\mathbf{x}_1) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \phi_1(\mathbf{x}_n) & \cdots & \phi_{M_{\phi}}(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}

Basis vector¶

\begin{equation} \phi(\mathbf{x}) = (\phi_1(\mathbf{x}), \phi_2(\mathbf{x}), \ldots , \phi_{M_{\phi}} ) \end{equation}

Vectorized model¶

\begin{equation} F_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w} \end{equation}

Penalised least-squares with basis functions¶

  • Penalised loss function:
\begin{eqnarray*} L(\mathbf{w}) & = & \sum_{i=1}^n \left( y_i - \sum_{m=1}^{M_\phi} w_m \phi_m( \mathbf{x}_i ) \right) ^ 2 + \frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{m=1}^{M_\phi} w^2_m \\ & = & (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w})^T (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w}) + \frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{w} \end{eqnarray*}
  • Solution
\begin{equation} \mathbf{w}^* = (\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi} + \lambda I)^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{y} \end{equation}

Kernelization¶

\begin{eqnarray} (\mathbf{\Phi^T} \mathbf{\Phi} + \lambda I) \mathbf{w}^* & = &\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{y} \\ \lambda \mathbf{w}^* & = & \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w}^* \\ & = & \mathbf{\Phi}^T ( y - \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w}^* ) \\ \mathbf{w}^* & = & \lambda^{-1}\mathbf{\Phi}^T(y - \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w}^* ) \\ & = & \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\alpha} \\ \end{eqnarray}

where $$\\\alpha_i = \lambda^{-1} \left[ y_i - \mathbf{w}^T \phi(\mathbf{x_i})\right]$$

\begin{eqnarray} \lambda \mathbf{\alpha} & = & \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w}^* \\ & = & \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \alpha \\ \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \alpha + \lambda \alpha & = & \mathbf{y} \\ \alpha & = & (\mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{\Phi}^T + \lambda I)^{-1} \mathbf{y} \\ & = & (K + \lambda I) ^ {-1} \mathbf{y} \\ \end{eqnarray}

Kernel matrix¶

\begin{equation} \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{\Phi}^T = \mathbf{K} = \begin{pmatrix} \phi(\mathbf{x}_1)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_1) & \phi(\mathbf{x}_1)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \phi(\mathbf{x}_1)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \phi(\mathbf{x}_2)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_1) & \phi(\mathbf{x}_2)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \phi(\mathbf{x}_2)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) & \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}

Kernelization¶

  • The non-kernelized optimal weight vector is:
\begin{equation} \mathbf{w}^* = (\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi} + \lambda I)^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{y} \end{equation}
  • In contrast the kernelized version is:
\begin{equation} \mathbf{w}^* = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\alpha} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T (\mathbf{K} + \lambda I)^{-1} \mathbf{y} \end{equation}

Kernalized predictions¶

\begin{eqnarray} \mathbf{F} & = & \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{w^*} \\ \mathbf{F}^T & = & \mathbf{w^*}^T \mathbf{\Phi}^T \\ & = & \mathbf{y}^T( \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{\Phi}^T + \lambda I)^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi^T} \mathbf{\Phi} \\ & = & \mathbf{y}^T(\mathbf{K} + \lambda I)^{-1} \mathbf{K} \end{eqnarray}

  • We do not require $\mathbf{\Phi}$.

  • Predictions can be made using only the $n \times n$ kernel matrix K.

Kernel in terms of features¶

\begin{equation} \mathbf{K} = \begin{pmatrix} \phi(\mathbf{x}_1)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_1) & \phi(\mathbf{x}_1)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \phi(\mathbf{x}_1)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \phi(\mathbf{x}_2)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_1) & \phi(\mathbf{x}_2)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \phi(\mathbf{x}_2)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) & \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_n) \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}

Using a kernel function¶

\begin{equation} \mathbf{K} = \begin{pmatrix} \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_1) & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_n) \\ \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_1) & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_n) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{x}_1) & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{x}_n) \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}
  • where $\operatorname{k}$ is a symmetric function such that $K$ is positive semi-definite $\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{K} \mathbf{x} \geq 0 \; \forall \mathbf{x}$.

The kernel trick¶

  • The linear kernel is simply the inner product:

$\operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \mathbf{x}_i ^T \cdot \mathbf{y}_j$

  • In this case our basis mapping is simply the identity function.

  • However, there are generalizations of the inner product called kernel functions.

  • Every kernel function defines an implicit set of basis functions.

Example¶

  • Consider an original feature space with $p=2$ dimensions, and with basis functions:
\begin{eqnarray*} \phi_1(\mathbf{x}) & = & x_1^2 \\ \phi_2(\mathbf{x}) & = & \sqrt{2} x_1 x_2 \\ \phi_3(\mathbf{x}) & = & x_2^2 \\ \end{eqnarray*}\begin{equation*} \phi(\mathbf{x}) = (x_1^2, \sqrt{2} x_1 x_2, x_2^2) \end{equation*}

The polynomial kernel¶

\begin{eqnarray*} \phi(\mathbf{x}_i)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_j) & = & x_{i1}^2 x_{j1}^2 + 2x_{i1}^2 x_{j1}^2 + x_{i2}^2 x_{j2}^2 \\ & = & (x_{i1}x_{j1} + x_{i2} x_{j2})^2 \\ & = & (\mathbf{x}^T_i \mathbf{x}_j)^2 \\ & = & \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \end{eqnarray*}

  • Our kernel matrix is defined only in terms of $\operatorname{k}$.

  • Using this kernel function we can work in the original 2-dimensional feature space instead of the 3-dimensional mapped feature space.

  • This is an example of a Polynomial kernel:

\begin{equation} \operatorname{k}(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = (\mathbf{x}^T_i \mathbf{x}_j + r)^d \end{equation}
  • For other kernels see Murphy (2012), ch. 14.

Out of sample forecasts¶

  • For an out of sample observation $\mathbf{x}_j$:
\begin{eqnarray} \hat{F} & = & \mathbf{w}^{*^T} \phi(\mathbf{x}_j) \\ & = & \sum_i^n \alpha_i \phi^T(\mathbf{x}_i) \phi(\mathbf{x}_j) \\ & = & \sum_i^n \alpha_i k(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \end{eqnarray}

Validating classifiers¶

  • Because data are qualitative as opposed to quantitative, we cannot use the same metrics, such as $R^2$ or out-of-sample $RSS$ that we used for regression methods.

  • We explicitly count false positives ($FP$), false negatives ($FN$), true positives ($TP$) and true negatives ($TN$).

  • For example, in a credit-risk model, a false positive would occur if we mis-classify an individual as high-risk, when in fact they were low-risk.

Accuracy¶

\begin{equation} accuracy = \frac{correct}{correct + incorrect} \end{equation}

Precision¶

\begin{equation} precision = \frac{TP}{TP + FP} \end{equation}

Recall (aka sensitivity)¶

\begin{equation} recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN} \end{equation}

Support Vector Machines¶

  • Support vector machines (SVMs) can be used to classify data into one of two classes (labels).

  • It is a supervised learning algorithm, so we start with known labels in our training data: for every training input $\mathbf{x}_i \in X$ we have a known label $y_i \in {+1, -1}$.

  • The task of the learning algorithm is to generalize to unseen cases not in our training set $X$.

  • The output from the classification is a definitive and non-probabilistic.

  • As in regression, we make use of a linear algebra to model the problem.

  • Non-linear problems can be solved using kernel functions.

Classification using hyperplanes¶

  • We can classify the data according to which side of the hyper-plan a data-point lies on.
\begin{eqnarray} \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \ldots + \beta_p X_p & > & 0 \\ \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \ldots + \beta_p X_p & < & 0 \\ \end{eqnarray}
  • That is, for a test observation $\mathbf{x}_j$ with $p$ features we can make predictions by examining the sign of:
\begin{equation} f(\mathbf{x}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{j1} + \beta_2 x_{j2} + \ldots + \beta_p x_{jp} \end{equation}

Maximising the margin¶

  • We can use $M$ to translate the classification into a loss-minimization problem with a unique solution.

  • First we will vectorize our equation for the hyperplane:

\begin{equation} \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = 0 \end{equation}

  • where the vector $\mathbf{w}$ is perpendicular to the plane.

  • For an observation $\mathbf{x}_i$ with label $y_i$, the distance to the hyperplane is:

\begin{equation} D_i = \frac{|\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b|}{||\mathbf{w}||_2} \end{equation}
  • The margin is the distance to the closest observation:
\begin{equation} M = \min_i D_i \end{equation}
  • The optimal model is the hyper-plane that gives us the best separation, i.e. the maximum margin:
\begin{equation} \mathbf{w}^* = \max_{\mathbf{w},b} M \end{equation}

Predictions¶

  • We can make predictions by examining the sign of $f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^T \cdot \mathbf{x} + b$.

  • To test against training data $\mathbf{y}$ we can examine the sign of the product $y_i f(\mathbf{x}_i)$.

  • Therefore the constraint that the classification is correct can be expressed:

\begin{equation} y_i[\mathbf{w}^T \cdot \mathbf{x} + b] \geq 0 \end{equation}

SVM as constrained optimization¶

  • Define the functional margin:
\begin{equation} M = \min_i y_i \left( \frac{\mathbf{w}}{ ||\mathbf{w}|| } \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + \frac{b}{||\mathbf{w}||} \right) \end{equation}
  • We want to solve
\begin{equation} \max_{\mathbf{w}, b} M \end{equation}

subject to:

\begin{equation} y_i \left( \frac{\mathbf{w}}{ ||\mathbf{w}|| } \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + \frac{b}{||\mathbf{w}||} \right) \geq M \; \forall i \end{equation}

Eliminating $M$¶

Define

\begin{equation} F = \min_i y_i ( \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b ) \end{equation}

Then the above constraint can be written:

\begin{equation} y_i ( \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b ) \geq F \; \forall i \end{equation}
  • We can normalize by rescaling $\mathbf{w}$ so that $F=1$ which gives us $M = \frac{1}{||\mathbf{w}||}$.

Reformulating as a quadratic¶

Our optimization problem is now:

\begin{equation} \max_{\mathbf{w},b} \frac{1}{||\mathbf{w}||} \end{equation}

subject to:

\begin{equation} y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot x + b) - 1 \geq 0 \; \forall i \end{equation}
  • This can be written as a quadratic optimization problem:
\begin{equation} \min_{\mathbf{w},b} \frac{1}{2} ||\mathbf{w}||^2 \end{equation}

subject to the same constraint:

\begin{equation} y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot x + b) - 1 \geq 0 \; \forall i \end{equation}

Softening the margins¶

\begin{equation} \min_{\mathbf{w},b} \frac{1}{2} ||\mathbf{w}||^2 + \frac{C}{n} \sum_i^n \xi_i \end{equation}

subject to constraints:

\begin{eqnarray*} y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot x + b) & \geq & 1 - \xi_i \; \forall i \\ \xi_i & \geq & 0 \; \forall i \\ \end{eqnarray*}

Standard form¶

\begin{equation} \min_{\mathbf{w},b} \frac{1}{2} ||\mathbf{w}||^2 + C \sum_i^n \xi_i \end{equation}

subject to constraints:

\begin{eqnarray*} 1 - \xi_i - y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot x + b) & \leq & 0 \; \forall i \\ - \xi_i & \leq & 0 \; \forall i \\ \end{eqnarray*}

KKT conditions¶

\begin{equation} \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i^* \nabla g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_{i=1}^p v_i^* \nabla h_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0 \end{equation}

\begin{equation} g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) \leq 0, \; i=1, \ldots, m \end{equation}\begin{equation} h_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0, \; \; i=1, \ldots, p \end{equation}\begin{equation} \lambda_i^* \geq 0, \; i=1, \ldots, m \end{equation}\begin{equation} \lambda_i^* g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0, \; i=1, \ldots, m \end{equation}

Lagrangian¶

Allocating Lagrange multipliers $\mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}$:

\begin{eqnarray} L(\mathbf{w}, b, \mathbf{\xi}, \mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) & = & \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{w} + \frac{C}{n} \sum_{i=1} \xi_i + \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i (1 - \xi_i - y_i(\mathbf{w}^T x_i + b)) - \sum_{i=1}^n \beta_i \xi_i \\ \end{eqnarray}

Lagrangian dual¶

\begin{equation} L_{D}(\mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) = \min_{\mathbf{w}, b, \xi_i} L(\mathbf{w}, b, \mathbf{\xi}, \mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) \end{equation}

The dual optimization problem is:

\begin{equation} \max_{\mathbf{w}, b, \xi_i} L_D(\mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) \end{equation}

KTT conditions¶

\begin{eqnarray} L(\mathbf{w}, b, \mathbf{\xi}, \mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) & = & \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{w} + \frac{C}{n} \sum_{i=1} \xi_i + \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i (1 - \xi_i - y_i(\mathbf{w}^T x_i + b)) - \sum_{i=1}^n \beta_i \xi_i \\ \end{eqnarray}\begin{equation} \nabla_\mathbf{w} L(\mathbf{w}, b, \mathbf{\xi}, \mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) = \mathbf{w} - \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i y_i \mathbf{x}_i = 0 \\ \end{equation}\begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial b} L(\mathbf{w}, b, \mathbf{\xi}, \mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) = - \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i y_i = 0 \end{equation}\begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial b} L(\mathbf{w}, b, \mathbf{\xi}, \mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) = \frac{C}{n} - \alpha_i - \beta_i = 0 \end{equation}

Substituting into the dual¶

\begin{equation} L_D(\mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}) = - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \mathbf{x}_i ^ T \mathbf{x}_j + \sum_{i}^n \alpha_i \end{equation}
  • We can now write the dual optimization problem as:
\begin{equation} \max_{\mathbf{\alpha}, \mathbf{\beta}} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i, j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \mathbf{x}_i^T \mathbf{x}_j + \sum_{i}^n \alpha_i \end{equation}

subject to:

\begin{eqnarray} \sum_{i} \alpha_i y_i & = & 0 \\ \alpha_i + \beta_i & = & \frac{C}{n} \\ \alpha_i, \beta_i & \geq & 0 \; i=1, \ldots, n \end{eqnarray}

As a quadratic programming problem¶

We an eliminate $\mathbf{\beta}$ to transform this into a quadratic programming problem:

\begin{equation} \max_{\mathbf{\alpha}} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \mathbf{x}_i^T \mathbf{x}_j + \sum_{i} \alpha_i \end{equation}

subject to:

\begin{eqnarray} \sum_i \alpha_i y_i & = & 0 \\ 0 \leq \alpha_i \leq \frac{C}{n} \; i=1,\ldots,n \\ \end{eqnarray}
  • This can be solved numerically using a quadratic optimizer to obtain $\mathbf{\alpha}^*$.

Optimal weights¶

  • From KKT condition 1:
\begin{equation} \mathbf{w}* - \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i^* y_i \mathbf{x}_i = 0 \end{equation}

Thus:

\begin{equation} \mathbf{w}* = \sum_{i=1} \alpha_i ^ * y_i \mathbf{x}_i \end{equation}

Support vectors¶

  • From KKT condition 5:
\begin{equation} \alpha_i ^ * ( 1 - \xi_i^* - y_i ( \mathbf{w}^{*^T} \mathbf{x}_i + b^*)) = 0 \end{equation}

  • The data that satisfy these constraints are the support vectors.

  • By the KKT conditions, $\alpha_i^* \neq 0$ i.f.f. $\mathbf{x}_i$ is a support vector.

  • In typical learning problems only a small fraction of the training inputs are support vectors $S$.

  • Our classifier has a compact representation:

\begin{equation} F(\mathbf{x}_j) = \operatorname{sign}( \sum_i \alpha_i^* y_i \mathbf{x}_j ^ T \mathbf{x}_i + b^*) \end{equation}

Inner products¶

  • The solution to the linear support-vector classifier optimization problem involves only the inner products of the observations:
\begin{equation} \langle x_i, x_{i'} \rangle = \sum_{j=1}^p x_{ij}x_{i'j} \end{equation}
  • The linear SVC can thus be represented:
\begin{equation} f(x) = \beta_0 + \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i \langle x, x_i \rangle \end{equation}

The kernel trick¶

  • A kernel is a function $\operatorname{k}(x_i, x_{i'})$ which quantifies the similarity of two observations:

  • The linear kernel is:

\begin{equation} \operatorname{k}(x_i, x_{i'}) = \sum_{j=1}^p x_{ij}{x_{i'j}} \end{equation}

  • If we use centered and scaled data, it is equivalent to the Pearson correlation.

  • We can use non-linear kernels, e.g. polynomial of degree $d$:

\begin{equation} \operatorname{k}(x_i, x_{i'}) = (1 + \sum_{j=1}^p x_{ij} x_{i'j})^d \end{equation}

Kernel-trick illustration¶

Consider

$$\phi(x) = (1, \sqrt{2}x_1, \sqrt{2}x_2, x_1^2, x_2 ^ 2, \sqrt{2}x_1x_2) \\ \phi(z) = (1, \sqrt{2}z_1, \sqrt{2} z_2, z_1^2, z_2 ^ 2, \sqrt{2}z_1 z_2)$$\begin{eqnarray} \phi(x) \cdot \phi(z) & = & 1 + 2 x_1 z_1 + 2 x_2 z_2 + x_1^2 z_1^2 + x_2 ^ 2 z_2 ^ 2 + 2 x_1 x_2 z_1 z_2 \\ & = & (1 + x_1 z_1 + x_2 z_2)^2 \\ & = & (1 + x.z)^2 \end{eqnarray}

The Bayes Classifier¶

  • The Bayes Classifier is the theoretically-ideal classifier.

  • We know a-priori the probability distributions for each class $P(X|Y=j)$.

  • We assign each observation to the most likely class, given its predictor values.

  • For a given data point $\mathbf{x}_i$ we choose the label $j$ for which maximises:

\begin{equation} Pr(Y = j | X = \mathbf{x}_i) \end{equation}

Bayes Rule¶

\begin{equation} P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)}{P(A)} \end{equation}
  • For a binary classifier with mutually exclusive labels $Y=0$ or $Y=1$:
\begin{eqnarray} Pr(Y = 1|X) & = & \frac{Pr(X|Y = 1) Pr(Y=1)}{Pr(X)} \\ \end{eqnarray}
  • Under the assumption that predictors are independent we can use:
\begin{equation} P(Y|X) = P(x_1 | Y) \times P(x_2|Y) \times \ldots \times P(x_n | Y) \times P(Y) \end{equation}
  • The above assumption is (sometimes) "naive", and gives us the name "Naive Bayes Classifier".

The Bayes Decision Boundary¶

  • For a binary classification problem with mutually exclusive labels $Y=0$ or $Y=1$ we use the Bayes decision boundary:

  • For a binary classification task with two labels $Pr(Y=1) = 1 - Pr(Y=0)$.

  • Therefore the decision boundary is $\{ \mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbf{X} : P(Y=1|X=\mathbf{x}_i) > 0.5 \}$.

\begin{equation} Pr(Y = 1 | X) > 0.5 \end{equation}
  • To illustrate, we first generate data using known conditional probabilities:

K-Nearest Neighbors¶

  • In general we do not know a-priori the actual conditional probabilities.

  • Therefore we have to estimate them.

  • With K-nearest neighbors (KNN) we first identify the $K$ points in the training data that are closest to a given training data $x_0$, e.g. using an Euclidian distance metric.

  • We then estimate the conditional probability as a sample mean:

\begin{equation} Pr(Y = j| X = x_0) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i \in N_0} I(y_i = j) \end{equation}

Logistic regression assumptions¶

  • We use a sigmoid function:
\begin{equation} \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \end{equation}
  • for a single training pair $(\mathbf{x}, y)$, we assume:
\begin{equation} P(Y=1 | \mathbf{X}=\mathbf{x}) = \sigma(z) \end{equation}

where:

\begin{equation} z = w_0 + \sum_{i=1}^m w_i x_i \end{equation}

Vectorized conditional probabilities¶

\begin{eqnarray} P(Y=1 | \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{x}) & = & \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}) \\ P(Y=0 | \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{x}) & = & 1 - \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}) \\ \end{eqnarray}

If the labels are 0 and 1, then $Y$ is a Bernoulli random variable $Y \sim Ber(p)$ where $p = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x})$, therefore:

\begin{equation} P(Y = y| X = \mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x})^y \cdot \left[1 - \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}) \right] ^{(1 - y)} \end{equation}

Likelihood function¶

\begin{eqnarray*} L(\mathbf{w}) & = & \prod_{i=1}^n P(Y = y_i | X = \mathbf{x}_i) \\ L(\mathbf{w}) & = & \prod_{i=1}^n \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)^y \cdot \left[1 - \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i) \right] ^{(1 - y)} \end{eqnarray*}

Log-likelihood function¶

\begin{eqnarray*} LL(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \log \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)^y \log \left[1 - \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i) \right] \end{eqnarray*}

  • To obtain optimal weights we maximise the above, which gives us the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE).

  • Provided that the data are not separated, this function is concave and can be solved numerically using gradient ascent.

Gradient function¶

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial LL(\mathbf{w})}{\partial w_j} = \sum_{i=1}^n \left[ y_i - \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i) \right] x_{ij} \end{equation}
